Power & air quality

On site power turns a data center into an air quality decision.

Power strategy determines fuel use, emissions, permits, noise, reliability, and whether impacts stay hidden behind the word “infrastructure.”

Data centers need reliable power every hour of the day. A facility that draws ordinary grid power still affects the electrical system. A facility that generates its own power on site creates additional local questions: what fuel is burned, how many turbines or engines are used, what pollutants are emitted, how emissions are modeled, and what happens during expansion.

Natural gas is often described as cleaner than coal, but “cleaner than coal” is not the same thing as free of impact. Gas combustion can produce nitrogen oxides, carbon dioxide, particulate related pollutants, and other air quality concerns depending on equipment and controls. Backup diesel generators can also matter because they must be tested and may run during emergencies.

If the power plant is inside the project fence, the emissions are part of the local public record.

Why air permits matter

An air permit is not paperwork. It is where a community can see projected emissions, equipment assumptions, operating limits, modeling, control technology, monitoring requirements, and public comment rights. For a project of this scale, residents should not have to wait until a draft permit appears to begin learning the issues.

The public should understand the difference between a gas turbine, a reciprocating engine, a diesel backup generator, a battery system, a renewable power agreement, and a transmission upgrade. Each has different costs, risks, emissions, noise, water use, lead times, and community impacts.

What Project Stratos makes urgent

Project materials state that Stratos is expected to produce its own power on site using natural gas. Independent analysis has examined a 9 gigawatt power scenario and estimated potentially large emissions and water use depending on the generation technology. The exact numbers must be tested against formal filings, but the public does not need to wait for final filings to understand the central issue: a data center powered by on site gas generation is not just a data center. It is also a major energy project.

Questions that should be answered before comfort language is accepted

1

Equipment

How many turbines, engines, backup generators, transformers, cooling towers, and substations are planned by phase?

2

Emissions

What are the expected NOx, CO2, particulate, and other emissions under normal operation, peak demand, startup, shutdown, testing, and emergency scenarios?

3

Monitoring

Will emissions, operating hours, fuel use, noise, and permit compliance data be public in a format residents can actually read?

4

Alternatives

What cleaner power, storage, transmission, efficiency, or phased load alternatives were studied before choosing on site gas generation?
