Water & cooling

Why “closed loop” is not the end of the water question.

Water questions include cooling, power generation, source water, discharge, drought, water rights, agriculture, wildlife, and long term basin impacts.

Data centers produce heat. The hotter the equipment runs, the more cooling the site needs. Some facilities use evaporative cooling, which can consume large amounts of water. Others use air cooling, closed loop systems, liquid cooling, or reclaimed water to reduce direct water demand. Those design choices matter, but none of them remove the public’s right to see the numbers.

When a project says it will use closed loop cooling, residents should ask what that means in engineering terms. How much water is needed to fill the system? How much makeup water is needed over time? What happens during heat waves? What water is used for fire protection, power generation, construction, dust control, landscaping, sanitation, and emergencies? What is discharged, where does it go, and what permits govern it?

The phrase “closed loop” should start the public review, not end it.

Water is local

Water withdrawn in one basin is not an abstract national average. It is water removed from a specific hydrologic system, under specific drought conditions, with specific neighbors, farms, wildlife, and downstream users. That is why a data center’s water source matters as much as its total volume.

In Box Elder County, residents are not only thinking about the data center building. They are thinking about groundwater, saline water claims, agriculture, the Great Salt Lake, Bear River systems, wetlands, wildlife, drought cycles, and whether future water right changes could create pressure that is not obvious on day one.

Power generation can also use water

Many people think data center water use only means cooling server rooms. But if a data center relies on on site power generation, the power plant itself may use water depending on the technology. Gas turbines, steam cycles, cooling systems, emissions controls, and industrial operations can create a separate water footprint from the data hall.

For Project Stratos, the public needs a clean separation between water used by the data center, water used by power generation, water used during construction, water used for fire protection, and water tied to any future permit or rights change. Combining all of those into one reassuring sentence makes the project harder to evaluate.

What residents should demand

1

Exact water source

Name the source, legal right, owner, quality, location, and whether any change application is needed.

2

Annual and peak demand

Show expected gallons by phase, by purpose, and by drought/heat scenario.

3

Cooling design

Explain the cooling method in plain language and disclose what happens when outside temperatures rise.

4

Discharge and water quality

Identify wastewater, brine, blowdown, stormwater, and any groundwater or surface water discharge path.
