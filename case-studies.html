Other communities

Box Elder is not the firstt community to face this.

Across the country, data center projects have raised the same core questions: water, power, noise, public costs, secrecy, and local control.

Other communities do not prove exactly what will happen in Box Elder County. But they do show what residents should ask before a project becomes too far along to question. The pattern is familiar: a large technology project promises investment and jobs, then residents discover that water, power, noise, tax incentives, and public records are more complicated than the first announcement made them sound.

The lesson is not that every data center should be rejected. The lesson is that communities need full information early enough to use it.

The Dalles, Oregon

Water use and public records became the fight.

In The Dalles, Google became the city’s largest water customer, and public reporting later showed hundreds of millions of gallons in annual water use. The public controversy was not only about the amount of water. It was also about disclosure: residents and journalists had to fight to understand how much water a major data center user was consuming.

For Box Elder, the lesson is direct: water claims should be specific, public, and understandable before approvals become momentum.

Virginia

Energy demand became a statewide planning problem.

Northern Virginia became the country’s best known data center hub. The benefits are real, but so are the planning problems. Virginia’s own legislative review found that modern data centers use far more energy than ordinary commercial or industrial operations and that unconstrained data center demand could require major new generation and transmission.

For Box Elder, the lesson is that “private investment” does not erase the need for public energy planning.

Newton County, Georgia

A single facility became a major water user.

In Newton County, reporting described a Meta data center using roughly 500,000 gallons of water per day, about 10 percent of the county’s water consumption. The larger issue was concentration: a use that may sound small nationally can become large inside one county water system.

For Box Elder, the lesson is that all water is local. A national average does not answer a basin level question.

Sterling, Virginia

Noise and on site power changed daily life.

Residents near a gas powered data center described constant noise that affected their homes and quality of life. The operators said they were compliant, but compliance did not make the daily experience disappear for neighbors.

For Box Elder, the lesson is that permit compliance and lived impact are not the same thing. Noise, setbacks, monitoring, and power strategy must be clear before operations begin.

The point of case studies is not fear. It is preparation.

Communities that ask late often get technical answers after the project is already shaped. Communities that ask early can demand better design, stronger monitoring, clearer records, enforceable commitments, better setbacks, cleaner power, public water accounting, and real comment periods.
